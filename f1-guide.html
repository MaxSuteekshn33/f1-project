What is Formula 1?

Formula 1 is the pinnacle of motorsport — the fastest, most technically advanced racing series on Earth. Twenty-two drivers from eleven teams compete in single-seater cars across 22–24 Grand Prix each year, visiting circuits in more than 20 countries. The 2026 season marks a landmark moment: an entirely new set of technical regulations — new engines, new aerodynamics, new power systems — making it the most radical overhaul of the sport in a decade.

The word "Formula" refers to the rules that all cars must follow. Every team builds their own car to the same technical regulations, so the battle is as much about engineering brilliance as driving skill. A single F1 car costs upwards of $15 million to build and can reach over 370 km/h (230 mph).

The Season
22–24 Races
Spread across March to December, visiting circuits on 5 continents. Two championships run in parallel — Drivers' and Constructors'.
The Teams
11 Constructors
Each team builds and races two cars. 2026 sees the arrival of Cadillac (General Motors) as the first new team since Haas in 2016 — and Sauber reborn as the Audi works team.
The Drivers
22 Drivers
Two per team. The grid expanded from 20 to 22 with Cadillac's entry — the largest grid since 2016. The top drivers earn over $50M per year.

How a Race Weekend Works

A Grand Prix weekend spans three days — Friday to Sunday — with a structured programme that builds toward the main event. Here's what happens and why it matters:

Friday
Free Practice 1 & 2 (FP1, FP2)
Teams run long-run pace tests and tyre compound comparisons. Engineers collect data to optimise car setup for qualifying. Rookies often drive in FP1 under regulations requiring teams to run a young driver.
Saturday AM
Free Practice 3 (FP3)
The last chance to fine-tune setup before qualifying. Teams run simulated qualifying laps to dial in the car. Usually 60 minutes.
Saturday PM
Qualifying — Q1 · Q2 · Q3
Q1 (18 min): All 22 cars run. The 6 slowest are eliminated — locking in grid positions 17–22. Q2 (15 min): The remaining 16 cars run. The 6 slowest are eliminated — filling grid spots 11–16. Q3 (12 min): The top 10 battle for pole position and the front of the grid. Starting first is a massive advantage because overtaking is so hard.
Sunday
The Grand Prix — Race Day
Typically 305 km (190 miles) long, taking around 90 minutes. Cars start in qualifying order and race wheel-to-wheel, making pit stops for tyres, managing fuel, and battling for every position. The first driver to complete the full distance wins.
Some
weekends
Sprint Race (6 weekends per year)
A shorter 100km race on Saturday that awards points to the top 8. Sprint qualifying replaces FP3 on Saturday morning. Fast, condensed racing with no mandatory pit stops.

The Points System

Points are awarded to the top 10 finishers in every Grand Prix. At the end of the season, the driver with the most points wins the Drivers' Championship. Each team's two drivers' points are combined for the Constructors' Championship, which is worth enormous prize money and prestige.

The maximum a driver can score in a standard race: 25 points. Across 24 races, that's a theoretical maximum of 600 points — nobody has ever come close. Note: the bonus point for fastest lap, introduced in 2019, was abolished from the 2025 season after the FIA felt it created more controversy than excitement.

The 2026 Technical Revolution

The 2026 season is the most technically disruptive in a generation. New power unit regulations, new aerodynamic philosophy, and two brand-new manufacturers have completely reset the playing field. Here's what's actually changed — and why it's been so controversial.

New Power Unit
The MGU-H is Gone
Since 2014, F1 cars have used a Motor Generator Unit – Heat (MGU-H) to harvest energy from the turbocharger. It was extraordinarily complex — one of the most sophisticated pieces of engineering in road or race cars — and gave Mercedes and Ferrari a years-long advantage that was nearly impossible to close. For 2026, the MGU-H has been completely abolished. The simplified system focuses entirely on the MGU-K (which harvests energy from braking) but makes it nearly three times more powerful: 350kW vs the old 120kW.
50/50 Power Split
Half Electric, Half Combustion
The 2026 power unit targets a 50/50 split between the internal combustion engine and the electrical system — a genuine step toward road relevance. Total output is around 1,000bhp. The cars also run on 100% sustainable fuel (advanced synthetic fuels). This is why Audi and General Motors (Cadillac) were attracted to enter: the technology is now close enough to what they develop for road cars to justify the investment.
Active Aerodynamics
The Wings Move Themselves
DRS (the old manually-operated rear wing flap) is gone. In its place: Active Aero. The front and rear wing elements automatically adjust their angle depending on where the car is on circuit — high downforce in corners, flat (low-drag) on straights. Combined with Overtake Mode (extra electrical deployment when within 1 second of a rival), the system is designed to make racing closer without the artificial feel of DRS.
The Controversy
Drivers & Teams Are Not Happy
The 2026 regulations have been among the most debated in F1 history. Max Verstappen publicly called the new cars "Formula E on steroids" and his least favourite generation to drive — arguing drivers will spend more time managing energy than actually racing. Several teams raised concerns that the extra electrical power deployment in slow corners could create unpredictable handling. The energy management element is real: on circuits with few long straights, the battery can run low, forcing drivers to "lift and coast" significantly.
New Manufacturers
Ford, Honda, Audi & GM Enter
Ford returns as Red Bull's engine partner (Red Bull Powertrains). Honda powers Aston Martin. Audi takes over the Sauber outfit as a full works team. General Motors backs Cadillac. The MGU-H removal was partly designed to lower the barrier to entry — the old unit was so complex it had deterred manufacturers for a decade. Ferrari and Mercedes remain as independent engine suppliers.
Narrower & Lighter
The Cars Look Different
2026 cars are narrower, lighter, and shorter than their predecessors. The overriding aesthetic difference is the wing behaviour — at high speed the wings go almost flat, giving the cars a sleeker, more raw appearance than recent years. Aerodynamic regulations were also rewritten to reduce the "dirty air" effect behind a car, in theory enabling closer wheel-to-wheel racing.

Race Strategy Explained

F1 isn't just about driving fast. The battle in the pits and on the radio is just as important as what happens on track. Understanding strategy turns a good race into a great one to watch.

Mandatory Rule
Tyre Compounds
Pirelli supplies three tyre compounds per race: Soft (fastest, wears quickly), Medium, and Hard (slowest, lasts longer). Drivers must use at least two compounds in a dry race. Choosing the right compound at the right time is one of the most important strategic decisions.
Where Time Is Made
Pit Stops
Teams can change four tyres in under 2 seconds. Pit stop timing determines whether you jump a rival (undercut) or get jumped (overcut). A 2.5-second stop versus a 3.5-second stop every race can be the difference between finishing P2 and P5 in the championship.
Overtaking Aid
Active Aero & Overtake Mode
DRS is gone from 2026. It's replaced by two linked systems. Active Aero automatically adjusts the front and rear wing angle between corners (high downforce) and straights (low drag/flat wing) for all cars. When within 1 second of the car ahead, a driver can activate Overtake Mode — deploying a burst of extra electrical power, similar in effect to the old DRS but driven by the hybrid battery rather than just a wing flap.
The Wild Card
Safety Car
When there's an accident or debris on track, a safety car comes out and bunches the field together. This erases gaps built up over laps and forces a free pit stop window — completely rewriting the race. The Virtual Safety Car (VSC) is a slower, digital version.
Rain Changes Everything
Wet Weather
Rain introduces Intermediate and Full Wet tyres. These completely reshuffle the competitive order — slower cars can beat faster ones, and driver skill in the wet matters far more than car pace. Monaco in the rain is pure chaos and spectacle.
Slipstream
Dirty Air
An F1 car creates turbulent air behind it. Following closely is called "running in dirty air" and makes a car harder to handle — it loses grip. This is why overtaking is so difficult even when a car is theoretically faster. The 2026 car regulations were redesigned again to further reduce dirty air — narrower cars, revised aerodynamics, and Active Aero all target the same goal: closer wheel-to-wheel racing.

The Legends

F1 history spans 75 years and dozens of champions. These are the drivers who defined eras — the ones fans argue about forever. Use the GOAT Simulator to put any two of them head to head.

1950s–1960s
Fangio & Clark
Juan Manuel Fangio won 5 championships in an era before seatbelts. Jim Clark won 2 with Lotus in the 1960s — considered by many the purest natural talent the sport has ever seen.
1970s–1980s
Lauda & Prost
Niki Lauda survived being burned alive to win a title the following year. Alain Prost — "The Professor" — won 4 championships through tactical genius rather than outright heroics.
The Rivalry
Senna vs Prost
The greatest rivalry in sport. Teammates at McLaren, they collided twice at the Japanese GP deciding the championship. Their war defined F1 in the late 1980s and still resonates today.
1990s Dominance
Schumacher
Michael Schumacher changed F1 forever. Seven championships, 91 wins, and a relentless approach to testing and development. His 2004 season (13 wins from 18 races) remains one of the most dominant ever.
Modern Era
Hamilton
Lewis Hamilton matched Schumacher's 7 titles and holds almost every record: 104 poles, 103 wins. The defining driver of the hybrid era who has also redefined what an F1 driver looks like off track.
Right Now
Verstappen
Max Verstappen's 2023 season (19 wins from 22 races) set a record that may never be beaten. He followed it with a fourth consecutive world title in 2024 — matching Vettel's run — before turning 27. Now entering the entirely new 2026 era, the debate is whether he can sustain dominance under radically different rules.

F1 Glossary

The F1 broadcast is full of jargon. Here's what it all means:

Box Box Box
Radio call telling the driver to come in for a pit stop. "Box" comes from "Boxengasse" — German for pit lane.
Undercut
Pitting early to get fresher (faster) tyres, then lapping quickly enough to come out ahead of a rival who hasn't pitted yet.
Overcut
The opposite — staying out longer than a rival who pits, then coming in later after they've got stuck in traffic on their out-laps.
Parc Fermé
French for "closed park." After qualifying, cars are locked and can't be significantly changed before the race. Teams must race the car as it was in Q3.
Tyre Deg / Degradation
How quickly a tyre loses performance. High-degradation tracks (like Bahrain) punish cars that are hard on rubber. Tyre management is a key skill.
Graining
When small chunks of rubber tear off the tyre surface, reducing grip. Often happens early in a stint before the tyre "comes in" to its operating window.
Blistering
When a tyre overheats and the rubber bubbles. More severe than graining and usually ruins the tyre for the rest of the stint.
Sector Times
Each circuit is divided into three sectors. Comparing sector times tells you exactly where on track a driver is gaining or losing time.
Gap to Pit Window
How far ahead a driver needs to be before pitting to emerge in front of a rival who stays out. Usually around 20–25 seconds.
Snap of Oversteer
When the rear of the car loses grip and slides out. Usually a moment of drama — sometimes it's "managed" by the driver; sometimes it's a spin.
Rake
The angle of the car — nose lower than rear. Different cars run different rake philosophies affecting how the aerodynamic package works.
FIA
Fédération Internationale de l'Automobile. The governing body that writes and enforces F1's rules. The stewards who hand out penalties report to the FIA.
Stewards
Race officials who investigate incidents and hand out time penalties, drive-through penalties, or grid drops. Their decisions are often controversial.
Constructor
The official term for a team in F1. They "construct" (build) their own cars. The Constructors' Championship is contested between the 10 teams.
MGU-K
Motor Generator Unit – Kinetic. Harvests energy from braking and deploys it as electrical power. In 2026 it delivers 350kW — nearly three times the power of the pre-2026 unit. The MGU-H (which harvested energy from the turbo) was abolished for 2026 to simplify the power unit and attract new manufacturers.
Overtake Mode
Replaced DRS from 2026. When within 1 second of the car ahead, a driver can activate a burst of extra electrical deployment from the MGU-K to gain top speed on straights — similar effect to DRS but powered by the battery rather than a wing flap.
Active Aero
A 2026 system where the front and rear wing elements automatically change angle based on speed and position on track. In corners the wings are fully loaded (maximum downforce); on straights they flatten out (minimum drag). Replaces DRS as the primary aerodynamic overtaking aid.
Delta Time
The time difference between a driver and a reference lap. Under the Safety Car, drivers must maintain a positive delta — they cannot go faster than the target time.

Where to Watch & Follow

Getting into F1 in 2026 has never been easier — and with a brand-new technical era underway, there's never been a better moment to start. Here's how to follow the sport:

Broadcast
F1 TV Pro
The official F1 streaming service. Watch every session live with onboard cameras, live timing, and multi-channel audio including team radio. The best way to experience F1 in full.
TV (USA)
ESPN
Broadcasts every race live on ESPN or ESPN2 in the United States. Sky Sports F1 covers every session live in the UK. Check local listings for your region.
Get Started
Drive to Survive
Netflix's documentary series follows F1 behind the scenes across a full season. Responsible for bringing millions of new fans to the sport. Start with Season 1 or 3 — both are excellent entry points.
Live Data
F1 Official App
Free live timing during race weekends, including position data, gap to leader, tyre compounds, and lap times. The best second-screen companion whether watching or listening.
Community
r/formula1
Reddit's F1 community is enormous and active. Great for race discussion threads, historical deep-dives, and understanding the sport from passionate fans who love to explain things.
Deep Dives
YouTube
Channels like WTF1, Chain Bear (technical explainers), and F1's own channel (with historical race highlights) will fill hours of watching.
